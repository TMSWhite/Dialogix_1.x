Background theoretical model - informal specification.

TRICEPS is a computational system designed in accordance with a model of inquiry/reasoning to generate new knowledge.

Knowledge

Knowledge, in general, is a systematic interaction of perception, association, and action. In most settings it occurs without any component of that rarest of elements, reason or inquiry, but is nonetheless visible in both the simplest reflex and the most complex system when, on the occasion of a discrete input, is there appears an associated output.


What does a basic traffic light know? It knows to turn from green to amber, amber to red, and red to green. It perceives only input from its timers. It makes only three associations. It behaves only by turning its lights on and off. It cannot learn to behave differently. It cannot conceive a new pattern of association. It cannot respond to anything besides the timer trigger.

Knowledge, in most settings, resides in a knower either by construction or imposition.


Alarm clocks know to ring at the appointed time because we set them to do so. Radar systems know to report the position of aircraft because someone built them that way. We know (can report) the value of pi, the latest box scores, or the neighbor's name by being told. We know how to tie our shoes by mimicry and repetition.

The limits of a system's ability to perceive, to associate, or to act and the limits of its ability to coordinate these activities, constitute the limits on its ability to know.

In what sense, then, can knowledge be given or acquired? Knowledge can be built in.


Knowledge, however obtained and within whatever limits, is demonstrable. That is, it is proveable, testable, validatable, measurable. It is not enough to point to a particular form, for example, the diagnostic criteria for major depression in DSM-IV, and say that is an example of psychiatric knowledge. It is at most a residue of knowledge and possibly a participant in knowledge. So when that information is part of a demonstrable behavior, say, in a psychiatrist making a diagnosis or in a resident passing the psychiatry boards, one recognizes knowledge. In knowledge, information is applied on an occasion where it is involved in the actual relationship between the knower and the thing known.

This restriction of the term knowledge is at odds with a good portion of its common usage where, for example, knowledge resides in books and knowledge is acquired and represented in the most diverse and inexact means. These "hypotheses of knowledge" however, are better not confused with occasions of knowledge.

Knowledge, as we consider it here, occurs in the application by a system of a particular mapping of three basic components, i.e., a perceived datum, an association (e.g., concept), and an action. It may result, on occasion, from a motivation to act in relation to a substance, object, or entity. If this mapping is not already stored in memory (known) then other known mappings and the execution of their specified actions are used to generate a new mapping. It is reference to and use of this mapping that achieves the transition from inquiring/reasoning state (of doubt) to a resolved state (of belief) in an inquirer/reasoner on occasions of knowing. This mapping is the particular way that a knower relates to the thing known in a particular context, or on a particular occasion. Knowledge, so specified, can be characterized as to its time, place, validity, complexity, accuracy, redundancy, ambiguity, scope, knower, and subject. Information is a term that best refers to those expressions that are processible in knowledgable systems.

Inquiry/Reasoning

Inquiry/Reasoning is the process of finding out, on the basis of what is already known, something else which is not known. It is performed by an inquirer/reasoner, on an occasion in which the inquirer/reasoner is motivated to address a problem, question, or doubt, about a specified substance, object, or entity (about which something may presumably be known), and results in either the establishment of a new, or the confirmation of a previous, disposition to behave in a particular way on similar occasions. The basic elements of inquiry/reasoning, then, are:

  • inquirer/reasoner

  • object of inquiry/reasoning

  • inquiring/reasoning state

  • resolved state

  • occasion

  • motivation

  • basis for inquiry/reasoning

  • behavior

Suppose a psychiatrist, already well-versed in the diagnostic criteria of DSM-IV, is asked the question, "Does the child Jean have ADHD?" Based on what the inquirer/reasoner already knows, she will procede to discover, through appropriate investigation, whether Jean has the findings that justify diagnosing ADHD. If asked the question again, the psychiatrist (unless a doubt arises about whether a change may have occured in Jean in the meantime) need not repeat the whole investigation but simply report the results previously discovered.

Inquirer/Reasoner

An inquirer/reasoner, as the agent in the above process, is either motivated to find something out (that is, in a state of doubt, inquiring/reasoning) or is already disposed to behave in a particular way (that is, already knows, is in a state of certainty or belief). In terms of the example above, if the psychiatrist does not know the answer, she knows how to go about finding it. The role played by an/reasoner requires that several processes and constructs be in place. The following are the most fundamental of these:

  1. Motivation: There must be a mechanism to motivate the inquirer/reasoner, that is, to place the inquirer/reasoner in a state of doubt from which there is an energeticstruggle to be freed (in the event that the inquirer/reasoner does not know what to do or is not disposed to act as the occasion requires).

  2. Interaction: There must be a means for the inquirer/reasoner to develop, store, and access mechanisms of interaction with the object of inquiry/reasoning, the thing itis supposed to know or in reference to which it must behave in a particular way.

  3. Conception: The inquirer/reasoner must have the means to develop, store, and access internal differentiae that (presumably) correspond to real and detectable differences in the object of knowledge.

  4. Episodic Memory: There must be a means of storing and accessing the separate episodes or events of interaction with any object of knowledge, both internal and external to the inquirer/reasoner.

  5. Resolution: There must be a mechanism to resolve the doubt and to mark the transition out of the state of doubt.

Object of inquiry/reasoning

A substance, object, or entity stands in a relationship with an inquirer/reasoner, at the very least, such that a real difference in its nature may correspond to a particular difference in the way an inquirer/reasoner is disposed to act towards it. The product of an inquiry/reasoning, whatever logical compulsion or practical necessity may have produced it, is valid just insofar as it reflects that correspondence. Incongruities in the relationship between what an inquirer/reasoner "knows" about an object and the actual characteristics of the object account for the fancies, redundancies, and ambiguities of our thinking. Incompleteness in that relationship is composed of both ignorance of what might possibly be known and whatever inherent limitations there might be on the possibilities of knowing at all under the circumstances. In terms of the above example, the primary object of inquiry/reasoning is Jean and whether or not she has ADHD. Of course, reference will be made, in the process of this inquiry/reasoning, to many other related objects, like diagnostic criteria and symptoms, that are either already known or will have to be found out, in order to resolve the main problem. It should be noted that the objects /reasoning are in many instances (and above a certain level of complexity, in most) internal to the inquiring/reasoning system, that is, elements of varying complexity stored in the inquirer/reasoner's own memory. It should also be noted that the source of the motivation, in the example, Jean's mother, must be something about which sufficient knowledge has been obtained so that the motivation makes sense.

Inquiring/Reasoning and Resolved States

When motivated on a particular occasion by a particular problem, question, or doubt, an inquirer/reasoner assumes a discrete, active state (a state of doubt or uncertainty) the primary aim of which is to resolve the issue and resume its resting state. A common product marking this transition is the equivalent of "I don't know." The subsequent "knowledge" the inquirer/reasoner has of the object, on the occasion of a similar motivation, is a certain, resolute ignorance from which she will be moved only with effort. On the other hand, there may be a more useful outcome of the process of inquiry/reasoning which will then mark the transition to a resting state. This latter state, accomplished with a useful outcome or not, unlike the active struggling of the inquiring/reasoning state, is quietly content to simply reproduce that outcome when the issue arises again. Returning to the above example, the psychiatrist, motivated by the question about Jean, who she does not know, is moved to a state of active inquiry/reasoning until she can resolve the issue by answering yes, no, or that she cannot find out at this time.

Occasion

An occasion, in general, is the scenario or context in which a problem is presented to an/reasoning agent to interact with respect to a substance, object, or entity in a particular way. If the agent is already disposed to act in a particular way on such occasions, that is, "knows" what to do or knows the answer, the necessary action ensues and the occasion is complete. If the agent does not know what to do, it may simply do nothing, it may be constructed in such a way as to report to the requesting entity that it doesn't know, or it may be an inquiring/reasoning agent to which the problem becomes a motivation to inquire and search for an answer. In the latter case, the inquiry/reasoning might proceed (subject to some internal or external termination criteria), to discover new knowledge, that is, to discover an effective way to act on such occasions. The occasion, in the example above, is the scenario in which a psychiatrist, a parent or caregiver, and a child are related in such a way that a question about the child's condition might meaningfully arise and be posed to the psychiatrist by the parent about the child.

Motivation

The motivation to inquiry/reasoning is the immediate trigger of the inquiring/reasoning or doubtful state. The occasion presents it in the form of an event that the inquirer/reasoner either cannot fully recognize or in respect to which does not know how to behave. It is often in the form of an expression commonly referred to as a problem, question, or doubt. Whatever the form, however, a motivation must contain elements that correspond to components of an extant knowledge mapping (see below) and at least one element (in the event that there is no mapping) that refers to the possible solution, the conclusion to be inferred, the unknown value. In the example above, the motivation to inquiry/reasoning is the question about Jean and ADHD. The answer to this question is not a matter of simple recall, a thing the psychiatrist knows as she must know the diagnostic criteria for ADHD. But it contains references to both known concepts (e.g., ADHD) and to unknown (e.g., Jean has ADHD) and so stimulates our psychiatrist into a state of inquiry/reasoning to find it out.

Basis for inquiry/reasoning

The basis for inquiry/reasoning (a knowledge base) is a system for the storage and retrieval of things already known. Knowledge includes both mappings of simple facts and mappings of rules that relate known things in such a way that a new thing might be known. These rules are the guiding principles of inquiry/reasoning. Among these principles are those that are built in from the outset. These given principles are integral to the inquiry/reasoning process and include the provided, fundamental structure and function of inquiry/reasoning. They include, for example, the ability to transit from inquiring/reasoning to resolved states, the definition of those states, the grammar of expressions, the rules of logic, the ability to access sources of information, and so on. Other principles, not taken for granted, are constructs either generated from acts of inquiry/reasoning or externally provided.

Behavior

The inquiring/reasoning agent behaves in accordance with what it knows or what it is motivated to learn. It either knows what a given occasion calls for (including a report of "don't know") or knows what to do to find out. The behavior is composed of actions in relation with the source of the motivation, with the object of inquiry/reasoning, and with the basis for inquiry/reasoning. Typical behaviors of a reasoning system include requests for information, either from internal memory or from outside data repositories, the composition and decomposition of concepts, ..... In our example, the expected behavior would be a report of some kind to the mother by the psychiatrist about Jean's condition. The next step might be to determine what do do about that condition, if anything.

The structure of TRICEPS is an instantiation of this model with components corresponding to those described above.


The "inquirer/reasoner" is the TRICEPS system itself. As mentioned above, there are several systematic and functional requirements that support the inquirer/reasoner role. Version 1 instantiates these in the following manner:

  1. Motivation: The system is motivated through a request (using HTTP) for the system's URL.

  2. Interaction: The system interacts with its "object of inquiry/reasoning" by composing a display to query the user and/or by accessing the value of generated nodes.

  3. Conception: Version 1 of the system instantiates the differentiae very simply, that is, in the form of unique strings corresponding to concepts in the DSM-IV and DISC domain.

  4. Episodic Memory: The system stores the datum produced through each interaction in association with the action that produced it and with its concept in a new node, a collection of which is stored as evidence. In version 1, the knowledge of the system is "primed" by composing the nodes that comprise the schedule. These nodes are theoretically the same as the new nodes generated by /reasoning (interview). Practically, and for now, however, they are handled separately.

  5. Resolution: The mechanism for doubt resolution in version 1 is the sequential consideration of all the nodes in the schedule. Allowance is made for suspending and resuming the inquiry/reasoning. Once the question is resolved, the session is completed.


The "object of inquiry/reasoning" has aspects that are pre-defined, definable by the author, and develop as the system generates new nodes. At a fundamental level it consists of the entities with which the system has been constructed to be able to interact. In version 1, this means with whoever provides input to the system through the GUI and with nodes stored in the system's own memory. As these interactions occur, that is, as new knowledge nodes are constructed, the objects of inquiry/reasoning may be much more defined and complex according to the qualities of the differentiated concepts and actions that are applicable. The system will then act, for example, with respect to not just any parent but Jean's parent or not with respect to ADHD but to the temporal pattern of a symptom that participates in the ADHD disorder. (In subsequent versions, the system will be able to interact at the more fundamental level with more "objects", e.g., with URLs via HTTP, external knowledge bases, or devices like cameras or microphones.)


The "inquiring/reasoning and resolved states" are instantiated in version 1 through evaluation of the action dependencies of each considered node. The whole system is in a resolved state once loaded by the web server where it awaits a request. Once a request arrives the system is in an inquiring/reasoning state until suspended or until the interview is completed (which presumably would answer the overarching question about the subject and ADHD). The sub-states of the system, as it handles the subsidiary actions implied by the overarching motivation, are instantiated in the consideration and resolution of each node in the schedule.


The system functions as a Java servlet in concert with a servlet-enabled web server. Version 1 takes advantage of the servlet session mechanism to provide a common logical context within which each part of the inquiry/reasoning takes place. In a general sense, this session is the "occasion" that characterizes the inquiry/reasoning. In a more detailed sense, however, each activation of a node is an occasion of knowledge and so there are, in the accomplishment of a DISC interview many such sub-occasions as each new node is generated.


The "motivation" is presented to the system both from outside and as a consequence of previously posed questions, that is, as implied from what is currently known. For example, the initial request for the system's URL corresponds to posing the question to the system, "Does the child Jean have ADHD?" The answer to this question requires the resolution of subsidiary questions (asked of the subject) like, "Do you dislike activities that requiring you to pay attention for long periods of time?" The inquiry/reasoning is complete when the top level question and all subsidiary questions are resolved. In Version 1, the initial motivation is coincident with the request for the URL and further motivation is determined by sequentially considering each node and evaluating its action dependencies.


The "basis for inquiry/reasoning" in the TRICEPS system is essentially composed by the discrete organization of concepts, actions, and data. In version 1 this is accomplished with the following components:

  1. the information stored in the spreadsheet that defines a schedule

  2. the system's action-parsing ability

  3. the data type handling

  4. the servlet and other Java-based functionality

  5. the generated nodes stored as evidence


Finally the system reports what it knows. The "knowledge" of a version 1 system is reported cumulatively during the course of an interview as answers to questions are given and as the system evaluates those answers and processes them.
